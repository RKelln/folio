# Grant Writing Craft

This document encodes the writing principles, workflow, and section-level rules that produce better grant applications. Read this entire document before writing. Then follow it.

## Pre-Writing Checklist

Before writing a single section, have these inputs. If any are missing, ask the user — do not guess.

### Must Have

- Full application form with all section prompts and character limits
- Funder's assessment criteria and their weighting
- Funder-specific rules
- Current organizational name, charitable number, addresses, fiscal year end
- Whether requesting an increase and the justification target

### Strongly Preferred (search the archive before writing)

- Most recent submitted application to any funder
- Current staff names, titles, bios
- Current board composition
- Membership/audience demographics (with percentages)
- Last 3 years of total revenues
- Current strategic plan
- Any known red flags: deficit, staff turnover, facility uncertainty

## Core Principle: Frame as a Support Organization

Every answer should be written from the position that your organization serves artists and the public. Programs, exhibitions, and services are *how* you serve — not what the organization is. Describe everything through the lens of who it serves and what gap it fills.

## Writing Workflow: Two Passes, Never One

### Pass 1: The Fact Draft

Goal: completeness and accuracy. Every claim grounded in a source document.
- Every number, date, name, and dollar figure traceable to a specific document
- Err on the side of including too much
- Do not worry about character count or narrative flow

### Pass 2: The Narrative Rewrite

Goal: a compelling, juror-accessible argument.
- Restructure freely: merge paragraphs, reorganize sections
- Every specific fact must remain correct and traceable
- Annotate uncertainties with `[CONFIRM: question]`

## The Juror Test

Before writing any section: "Would a juror who has never heard of my organization understand this paragraph?"

If the answer is no, rewrite it.

## Section-Level Rules

Every section must pass the "So What" test: a section that lists activities fails. A section that explains what each activity does for artists and what gap it fills passes.

Every section must include specifics: vague claims are invisible to jurors.

## Anti-Patterns

1. Catalogue mode — listing everything without explaining why it matters
2. No bridge between sections — each should flow naturally into the next
3. Facts without framing — numbers without context are noise
4. Corporate-speak — write in plain English
5. Assuming the juror knows your art form — explain briefly
6. Burying the lead — state the most important point first
7. Vague claims of impact — use concrete evidence
8. Treating the application as one document — sections may be read in any order
9. Defending Pass 1 structure — restructure freely for narrative quality
10. Missing inline confirmation markers — tag uncertainties inline
11. Stripping staff credentials — preserve degrees, board service, prior roles
12. Hedging on facts — never use "appears to," "seems to," "may reflect"
13. Repeating the same framing device — each section makes its own argument
14. Under-using available space — below 60% of limit, review for omitted specifics
15. Making the funding request feel optional — build the case so the increase feels structurally inevitable
